Machine Learning approach to solve trait-matching problem: impediments and possibilities

Master's Thesis

Abstract

The goal of trait-matching, a method for solving problems in many scientific do-mains, is to predict how well two different kinds of objects match by analyzing their distinctive characteristics. Machine Learning (ML) models are often utilized for this purpose to predict interactions between objects based on their features or traits. It is also important to comprehend the features predominantly used by these models to understand whether they represent the actual problem. Pichler et al. (2020) explored ML models and XAI for prediction and inference in trait-matching. However, it is crucial to explore how challenges such as the uneven abundance distribution of species like the Harry Potter problem or class imbalance impair prediction and infer-ence. Predictions are skewed in imbalanced datasets, which frequently arise when one class is less common than the dominant one. Additionally, it investigates how Machine Learning techniques are affected by collinearity between two traits. To pre-dict an interaction under feature collinearity and abundance issues, this thesis exam-ines how well explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) techniques, such as the Average Marginal Effect, explain the interaction between traits. I found DNN outperforms Random Forest and GLM in terms of both predictive and inferential performance. However, with the correct model, GLM can perform better than Random Forest and DNN. Since the selection of the perfect model is challenging in real-world scenarios, this study identifies DNN combined with the Average Marginal Effect as a promising approach to address trait-matching problems.

simulatr

An R package for simulating omics datasets either from scratch or from existing, publicly available datasets designed for researchers and data scientists who need reliable synthetic datasets. Configurable parameters are correlation structure, biases, noise and the relationship between predictors and outcome variable. Evaluation of statistical methods is best done with datasets where all relevant parameters can be controlled. Parameters of interest are e.g. the number of observations/features, the correlation structure between samples/features, the relationship between features and outcome and the amount of noise and/or biases within the data. As this is rarely possible with real-world datasets, it often makes sense to not only evaluate methods on real-world datasets, but also on simulated datasets. The goal of this package is therefore, to make simulation of such datasets as easy as possible.

BucketFuser

Bucket Fuser (BF) is a novel 1D NMR feature extraction procedure based on a regularized regression framework with fused group LASSO terms. BF dynamically constructs NMR metabolite features. The width of the generated buckets can be adjusted via a regularization parameter. In this project I have used Bucket Fuser to extract features from AKI dataset. Acute kidney injury (AKI) is a frequent complication after cardiopulmonary bypass. In this AKI dataset, nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy is employed in a prospective study of adult cardiac surgery patients to identify metabolites prognostic of AKI in plasma specimens collected 24h after surgery. Postoperative AKI of stages 1 to 3 was considered by the Acute Kidney Injury Network (AKIN). In this project, I have analyzed the performance of Bucket Fuser with AKI dataset.
